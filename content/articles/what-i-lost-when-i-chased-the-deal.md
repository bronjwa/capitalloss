There is a version of this story where everything works out. The deal closes. The partnership scales. The friendship deepens through the shared work of building something together.

That is not this story.

Daniela had known Priya since college. They had been close in the way that some friendships are — close enough that distance and time did not erode them, close enough that the friendship felt like a permanent thing, like infrastructure.

When they decided to go into business together, it felt natural. They were both experienced. Both successful in their own right. Both old enough to know how to have difficult conversations.

Neither of them anticipated what the negotiation would reveal.

## Equity and Everything Underneath It

They agreed on the concept quickly. A consulting firm targeting mid-market brands in the consumer goods space. Daniela had the operational background. Priya had the relationships and the sales instinct.

The equity conversation is where the friendship started to show its fractures.

Daniela wanted a fifty-fifty split. Priya wanted sixty-forty in her favor — the relationships, she argued, were the business. The clients would come because of her.

It is a reasonable argument. It is also the kind of argument that reveals something about how a person sees themselves in relation to others.

> "I realized that her idea of what she was worth, and her idea of what I was worth, were very different from mine. And we had never needed to make that explicit before."

The negotiation lasted three weeks. They went through lawyers, which added a formality that felt like a kind of violence against what the friendship had been. They eventually reached a fifty-five / forty-five agreement — not the fifty-fifty Daniela wanted, not the sixty-forty Priya had pushed for.

Both of them signed. Neither of them felt good about it.

## The Business That Never Started

They never actually launched.

The lawyers' fees had introduced a chill. The negotiation had introduced a knowledge problem — they now both knew things about how the other person valued the relationship that could not be unknown.

Within two months of signing the partnership agreement, Daniela proposed an exit. Priya agreed. They settled the legal structure and went their separate ways professionally.

The friendship never recovered the way it had been. They still talk. They are still, in some technical sense, friends. But the intimacy is gone — replaced by something more careful, more measured, more aware of what the other person might want from you.

## What the Loss Actually Was

The business loss was minimal. They had not yet launched, had not yet taken on clients. The legal fees were real — roughly eleven thousand dollars between them — but that was the extent of the financial damage.

The actual loss was the friendship in its original form. The kind of friendship that existed before they knew how each other negotiated. Before the spreadsheets and the lawyers and the uncomfortable truth that emerged from the process of turning something informal into something contractual.

Business partnerships, when they go wrong, do not just cost money. They cost the relationship that existed before the business conversation started.

Sometimes you cannot get that back. Not fully. Not the way it was.

That is the cost Daniela thinks about most. Not the eleven thousand dollars.
