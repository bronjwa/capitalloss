Simone had been making music independently for six years. She released four projects — not to large audiences, but to devoted ones. The kind of small following that feels like a real thing, like something alive.

She was also, by most measures, broke. And tired. And watching people with less interesting music do better because they understood a side of the industry that she had always refused to learn.

So when the manager reached out, she said yes.

## The Access

The first few months were exciting in the specific way that access is exciting before you understand what it costs.

She took meetings. She sat across from A&R people at labels. She had conversations about "the plan" and "positioning" and "what the story is." She learned a vocabulary she had not previously possessed.

She also, quietly, stopped making music.

It was not a conscious decision. There was no morning where she sat down and chose to stop creating. It happened gradually, through accumulation — through the scheduling, the travel, the mental bandwidth consumed by industry relationships, through the subtle pressure to wait until the situation was "sorted" before putting new work out.

> "I kept being told to hold back. Don't release until we know where this is going. Every time I wanted to make something, there was a reason to wait."

The waiting calcified into something heavier. By month six, the creative muscle had atrophied in a way that surprised her. She would sit with her instruments and feel nothing. Not blocked, exactly. More like estranged. As if the person who made those four projects was someone she used to know.

## What the Year Produced

The management relationship dissolved after fourteen months. The label conversations had gone nowhere definitive. There was no deal. There was nothing to release. There was a version of her name in a few people's inboxes and a collection of half-finished sessions she could not stand to listen back to.

And there was the audience she had built — slowly, genuinely, over six years — that had gone a year without hearing from her. Some of them had drifted. Not all. But some.

## The Actual Inventory

The loss was specific and recoverable, but only partially.

She got back to making music. It took three months of what she describes as "relearning how to be alone with it without needing it to go anywhere." The fourth project she made after that period — the one she released independently again — is her best work.

But she is honest about what she spent.

A year. A particular version of momentum that does not rebuild the same way. The audience trust that comes from consistency, which you cannot buy back, only rebuild slowly. And something harder to name — the belief she had carried that the work was enough on its own, which the year had shaken in ways she still feels.

The industry access was real. It just cost something that the access was never going to give back.

She thinks about that trade differently now. Not with bitterness, mostly. But with a clarity she did not have before.

The decision to wait was a decision. The year without making things was a choice, even when it did not feel like one.

That is the lesson she keeps.
